The Core Shift: From Past Causes to Present Goals

This section explores the fundamental difference between traditional Freudian etiology (study of causation) and Adlerian teleology (study of purpose). Understanding this shift is the first step toward personal freedom. Interact with the buttons below to see how each philosophy interprets the same issue.

Deep Dive: Freedom From the Past

Adler's most radical idea is that the past has no causal power. We are not pushed by our history; we are pulled by our goals. This section further breaks down the logic of etiology and teleology to show how you can reclaim power over your life story.

Deep Dive: True Belonging

Adlerian psychology does not advocate for isolation. It aims for true social integration through "Community Feeling." This is the sense of having a place and being of value. It is built on the following three pillars and a shift from vertical to horizontal relationships.

1. Self-Acceptance

This is not about self-affirmation ("I am great!"). It is about calmly accepting your imperfect self as-is. If you score a 60 out of 100, you accept the 60 and focus on how to get to 61, rather than lamenting you are not at 100.

2. Confidence in Others

This means having unconditional trust in your fellow human beings. You see them as your comrades, not your enemies or rivals. This allows you to build horizontal relationships based on mutual respect, not competition.

3. Contribution to Others

True happiness is the feeling of contribution. This does not mean self-sacrifice. It means feeling "I am useful to someone," which gives you a profound sense of your own value and a place in the community.

About the Authors

The book is a unique collaboration between a philosopher and a writer, blending deep psychological insight with an accessible, engaging narrative style.

Ichiro Kishimi

Born in 1956, Kishimi is a philosopher, lecturer, and certified counselor of the Japanese Society of Adlerian Psychology. He has written numerous books on philosophy and Adlerian psychology, aiming to make its principles practical for a modern audience. He is considered one of Japan's foremost experts on the subject.

Other Notable Works:

  • The Courage to be Happy (sequel)
  • Adler's Psychology Lesson
  • Don't Be Liked by Everyone

Fumitake Koga

Born in 1973, Koga is an award-winning professional writer and author. He encountered Adlerian psychology in his late twenties and was deeply moved by its ideas. He sought out Ichiro Kishimi to transcribe his teachings into the dialogue format found in the book, playing the role of the "Youth" to make the concepts clear and relatable.

Other Notable Works:

  • The Courage to be Happy (sequel)
  • 20-dai ni shite okitai 17 no koto
  • Taking Notes: The Secret of an Intellectual Production
