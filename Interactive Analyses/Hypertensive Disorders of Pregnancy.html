A Global Health Imperative

This section provides a high-level introduction to Hypertensive Disorders of Pregnancy (HDP). You will learn about the prevalence of these conditions and why they are considered a critical marker for a woman's long-term cardiovascular health. The chart below visualizes the significant impact of HDP on pregnancies worldwide.

Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (HDP) are a group of conditions that represent one of the leading causes of maternal and infant illness and death globally. Their prevalence is rising due to factors like increased maternal age and pre-existing health conditions.

Crucially, HDP is not just a temporary issue during pregnancy. It's now understood as an early warning sign for a woman's future risk of developing chronic hypertension and other cardiovascular diseases. This shifts the focus from a short-term obstetric problem to a long-term health journey that requires care far beyond delivery.

Key Takeaway:

HDP is a "stress test" for the cardiovascular system, unmasking a woman's predisposition to future heart and vascular disease.

The Spectrum of HDP

HDP is not a single condition but a spectrum of disorders. Accurate classification is key to proper management. In this section, you can interactively explore the four primary types of HDP. Click on each tab to understand its specific definition, timing of onset, and key characteristics. This allows for a clear comparison of how these related but distinct conditions are identified.

Chronic Hypertension

High blood pressure (BP ≥ 140/90 mmHg) that exists before pregnancy, is diagnosed before 20 weeks of gestation, or persists more than 12 weeks after delivery. It complicates 1-5% of pregnancies and is a major risk factor for developing superimposed preeclampsia.

Gestational Hypertension (GH)

New onset of high blood pressure (BP ≥ 140/90 mmHg) at or after 20 weeks of gestation without proteinuria or other signs of end-organ damage. It's often a provisional diagnosis, as up to 50% of women with GH will go on to develop preeclampsia.

Preeclampsia-Eclampsia

A complex, multi-system disorder defined by new-onset hypertension after 20 weeks with proteinuria OR, in its absence, signs of significant end-organ damage (e.g., low platelets, impaired liver/kidney function, severe headaches). Eclampsia is the onset of seizures in a woman with preeclampsia.

Superimposed Preeclampsia

Occurs when a woman with pre-existing chronic hypertension develops new-onset proteinuria or other severe features of preeclampsia after 20 weeks. This combination carries a markedly higher risk for mother and baby.

The Two-Stage Model of Preeclampsia

To understand preeclampsia, it's essential to know its origin story, which begins with the placenta. This section visually breaks down the widely accepted "Two-Stage Model." It illustrates how early, silent problems with placental development lead to the widespread maternal symptoms that appear later in pregnancy. This cause-and-effect flow helps demystify how a placental issue can impact the entire body.

Stage 1: Defective Placentation (Early Pregnancy)

1️⃣

Poor Spiral Artery Remodeling

In a normal pregnancy, maternal arteries remodel to become wide, low-resistance vessels. In preeclampsia, this process is incomplete, leaving narrow, constricted arteries.

2️⃣

Placental Ischemia & Stress

The narrow arteries lead to reduced blood flow (ischemia) and oxidative stress in the placenta. This stage is clinically silent.

Stage 2: Maternal Syndrome (Late Pregnancy)

3️⃣

Release of Toxic Factors

The stressed placenta releases anti-angiogenic factors (like sFlt-1) into the mother's bloodstream.

4️⃣

Systemic Endothelial Dysfunction

These factors attack the lining of the mother's blood vessels (endothelium) throughout her body, causing hypertension, kidney damage (proteinuria), liver issues, and neurological symptoms.

Who is at Risk?

Identifying risk factors is key to prevention. This section outlines the high and moderate-risk factors for developing preeclampsia. The chart visually compares the impact of several key factors, while the lists provide a comprehensive overview. This dual presentation helps in understanding both the magnitude and the breadth of risk, which guides preventative strategies like low-dose aspirin therapy.

High-Risk Factors (≥1 factor warrants prevention)

  • History of Preeclampsia
  • Multifetal gestation (twins, etc.)
  • Chronic hypertension
  • Type 1 or 2 Diabetes
  • Pre-existing kidney disease
  • Autoimmune diseases (e.g., Lupus, APS)

Moderate-Risk Factors (≥2 factors warrant prevention)

  • First pregnancy (Nulliparity)
  • Obesity (BMI > 30)
  • Family history of preeclampsia
  • Advanced maternal age (≥35 years)
  • Black race (as a proxy for racism's impact)
  • In vitro fertilization (IVF)

Key Symptoms of Preeclampsia Progression

While many women are asymptomatic, the following new-onset symptoms in the second half of pregnancy require immediate medical attention:

  • 🧠
    Severe Headache: Persistent, throbbing, and unresponsive to standard pain relief.
  • 👁️
    Visual Disturbances: Blurred vision, flashing lights, or dark spots.
  • 🤢
    Upper Abdominal Pain: Severe pain in the upper right or middle abdomen, under the ribs.
  • 🎈
    Sudden Swelling: Rapid swelling, especially in the face and hands; sudden weight gain.
  • 😮‍💨
    Shortness of Breath: Difficulty breathing can be a sign of fluid in the lungs (pulmonary edema).

The "Preeclampsia Panel" & Workup

Diagnosis relies on a combination of measurements and lab tests to assess for hypertension and end-organ damage.

  • Blood Pressure: ≥140/90 mmHg on two occasions, or ≥160/110 mmHg for severe range.
  • Urine Protein: Urine protein/creatinine ratio ≥ 0.3 or a 24-hour collection ≥ 300 mg.
  • Platelet Count: Low platelets (<100,000/μL) indicate a severe feature.
  • Liver Function: Elevated liver enzymes (AST/ALT) to twice the normal limit.
  • Kidney Function: Elevated serum creatinine (>1.1 mg/dL or doubling of baseline).

The Care Pathway

Management of HDP is a journey that begins with prevention and continues long after delivery. This interactive timeline outlines the key stages of care. Click on each stage to reveal the primary goals and interventions, from preventative low-dose aspirin for at-risk women, to intensive monitoring during pregnancy, and crucial surveillance in the "fourth trimester" postpartum.

1. Prevention (Pre-16 Weeks)

For women with high or moderate risk factors, specific interventions can significantly reduce the chance of developing preeclampsia.

  • Low-Dose Aspirin (81mg): The most effective therapy. Recommended for those with ≥1 high-risk or ≥2 moderate-risk factors, started before 16 weeks.
  • Calcium Supplementation: Recommended by WHO for women with low dietary calcium intake.
  • Lifestyle: Exercise and healthy weight management are key primary prevention strategies.

2. Antenatal Management (During Pregnancy)

Once HDP develops, management is tailored to the type, severity, and gestational age, balancing maternal safety with fetal well-being.

  • Chronic/Gestational HTN: Treatment with safe medications (Labetalol, Nifedipine) if BP ≥ 140/90 mmHg. Regular maternal and fetal surveillance. Delivery typically around 37-39 weeks.
  • Preeclampsia w/o Severe Features: Intensive outpatient surveillance. Delivery recommended at 37 weeks.
  • Preeclampsia w/ Severe Features: Hospitalization is mandatory. Treatment includes IV magnesium sulfate for seizure prevention and IV antihypertensives for BP control. Delivery is the only cure, planned after stabilization (at 34 weeks or later, or immediately if unstable).

3. Postpartum & Long-Term Care (The "Fourth Trimester" & Beyond)

The risk doesn't end at delivery. Blood pressure often peaks 3-7 days postpartum, making this a high-risk period.

  • Immediate Follow-up: ACOG recommends a BP check within 7-10 days postpartum (or 72 hours for severe cases). Remote BP monitoring is an effective strategy.
  • Patient Education: Women must be counseled that HDP is a major risk factor for future cardiovascular disease.
  • Lifelong Surveillance: Annual checks of blood pressure, lipids, and glucose are recommended, along with a formal transition of care to a primary provider.
