Defining the Deception

This section provides a foundational understanding of Factitious Disorder Imposed on Self (FDIS), formerly known as Munchausen Syndrome. It explores the disorder's core concept: the compulsive, internal need to assume the "sick role" for psychological gain, distinguishing it from other conditions. You will learn about the evolution of its terminology and why this shift is clinically significant for diagnosis and treatment.

Factitious Disorder Imposed on Self (FDIS)

A serious psychiatric condition where an individual intentionally fabricates, exaggerates, or induces physical or psychological symptoms in themselves. The primary motivation is not for external gain (like money or avoiding work), but to assume the "sick role" to receive medical attention, care, and sympathy. The behaviors are conscious and deliberate, but the underlying compulsion is driven by deep-seated, often unconscious, psychological needs.

The Portrait of Fabricated Illness

Here, we delve into the specific behaviors and methods used by individuals with FDIS to create the appearance of illness. This section details the spectrum of feigned conditions, the behavioral hallmarks like "doctor shopping," and the three primary methods of deception. An interactive element allows you to explore each method—from simple falsification to dangerous self-harm—to understand the calculated and often extreme nature of the disorder.

Falsification: Lying and Inventing

The most basic level of deception. Individuals lie about symptoms (e.g., pain, seizures), invent complex medical histories, claim treatment in other countries to explain a lack of records, and may even forge medical documents to support their fabricated stories.

Tampering: Manipulating Tests

This involves actively manipulating diagnostic processes. Common examples include heating a thermometer to fake a fever or contaminating a urine sample with blood or glucose to suggest kidney disease or diabetes, thereby misleading clinicians with false objective data.

Self-Infliction: Inducing Illness

The most dangerous form, where individuals deliberately harm themselves to produce genuine signs of illness. This can include injecting bacteria or feces to cause infections, taking unprescribed medications like blood thinners to mimic bleeding disorders, or aggravating wounds to prevent healing.

A Darker Side: Factitious Disorder Imposed on Another

This section addresses a separate but related disorder, Factitious Disorder Imposed on Another (FDIA), formerly Munchausen by proxy. It is crucial to understand that FDIA is not a variant of FDIS but a severe form of abuse. Here we define the disorder, profile the typical perpetrator and victim, and detail the dangerous behaviors that place vulnerable individuals at extreme risk.

FDIA: A Form of Abuse

In FDIA, a caregiver deliberately fabricates, exaggerates, or induces illness in a person under their care (often a child). The psychiatric diagnosis is assigned to the perpetrator, while the victim is diagnosed as a subject of abuse. The perpetrator's motivation is similar to FDIS—to gain attention and sympathy—but the harm is inflicted on another, making this a critical issue of safety and mandatory reporting.
