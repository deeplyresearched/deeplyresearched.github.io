A Systemic Autoimmune Disease

Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA) is more than just joint pain. It's a chronic autoimmune disease where the body's immune system mistakenly attacks its own healthy tissues, primarily the lining of the joints. This interactive guide explores the nature of RA, how it's diagnosed and treated, and its impact on the entire body. A crucial first step is distinguishing it from Osteoarthritis (OA), a "wear-and-tear" condition.

Who is at Risk?

The development of RA is a complex interplay between genetics and environmental factors. While some factors like age and family history are unchangeable, others related to lifestyle can be modified to reduce risk. Understanding these factors is key to grasping the disease's origins.

Symptoms & Diagnosis

RA typically manifests with joint-related (articular) symptoms and body-wide (extra-articular) signs. Early diagnosis is a race against time, as prompt treatment can prevent irreversible damage. Key Symptoms

  • Symmetrical Swelling: Affects the same joints on both sides of the body.
  • Prolonged Stiffness: Morning stiffness lasting over 45 minutes.
  • Systemic Fatigue: Profound lack of energy, often with low-grade fever.
  • Pain and Warmth: Affected joints are painful, swollen, and warm to the touch.

Diagnostic Clues

  • Blood Tests: Look for Rheumatoid Factor (RF) and highly specific anti-CCP antibodies.
  • Inflammatory Markers: Elevated ESR and CRP levels indicate active inflammation.
  • Imaging: Ultrasound and MRI can detect early inflammation, while X-rays show later-stage bone erosion.

Modern Treatment Strategies

The goal of modern RA management is to achieve remission or low disease activity through a "Treat-to-Target" approach. This involves using Disease-Modifying Antirheumatic Drugs (DMARDs) to control the underlying disease process, not just the symptoms. Explore the major classes of these powerful medications.

The Systemic Impact of RA

The Cardiovascular Connection

RA is an independent risk factor for heart attack and stroke, on par with type 2 diabetes. Controlling RA inflammation is critical for protecting the heart.
