Understanding Thyroid Function

Your thyroid is your body's master metabolic regulator. This application provides a comparative look at the two most common forms of thyroid dysfunction: Hypothyroidism (an underactive thyroid) and Hyperthyroidism (an overactive thyroid). Explore the sections to understand their causes, symptoms, and treatments.

The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Thyroid (HPT) Axis

Your body maintains thyroid hormone levels through a sensitive feedback loop. The brain (hypothalamus and pituitary) tells the thyroid to work. The thyroid hormones then signal the brain to slow down, creating a perfect balance. Autoimmune diseases disrupt this balance.

Approaches to Treatment

Treatment goals are to restore normal hormone levels and relieve symptoms. The strategies for hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism are fundamentally different, reflecting their opposite nature.

Treating Hypothyroidism

The goal is simple: replace the missing hormone. This is achieved with a synthetic thyroid hormone pill, taken daily.

Levothyroxine (L-T4)

The standard of care. It's a synthetic version of the T4 hormone. The dose is carefully adjusted based on TSH blood tests to find the perfect level for each individual. Treatment is typically lifelong.

Treating Hyperthyroidism

The goal is more complex: reduce the overproduction of thyroid hormone. There are three main approaches, and the choice depends on many factors, including patient preference.

Antithyroid Drugs (ATDs)

Medications like Methimazole block the thyroid's ability to produce new hormones. This approach is not permanent and is often used for 12-18 months to see if the condition goes into remission.

Radioactive Iodine (RAI)

A single dose of radioactive iodine is taken, which is absorbed by and destroys the overactive thyroid cells. This is a permanent solution that usually leads to hypothyroidism, requiring lifelong hormone replacement.

Thyroidectomy (Surgery)

Surgical removal of the thyroid gland. This provides the quickest, most definitive cure for hyperthyroidism but also results in permanent hypothyroidism requiring lifelong hormone replacement.
