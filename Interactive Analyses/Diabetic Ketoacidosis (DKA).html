Diagnosis & Differentiation

A rapid and accurate diagnosis is critical in managing a hyperglycemic crisis. This section outlines the key laboratory findings and calculations needed to confirm DKA. It also provides a clear comparison to its main differential diagnosis, Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS), to aid in immediate clinical differentiation.

Key Diagnostic Workup

  • Serum Glucose: Typically >250 mg/dL. Beware of Euglycemic DKA.
  • Serum Ketones: β-hydroxybutyrate ≥3.0 mmol/L is the gold standard.
  • Venous/Arterial Blood Gas: pH < 7.3, Bicarbonate < 18 mEq/L.
  • Anion Gap Calculation: Essential for confirming high anion gap metabolic acidosis. AG > 12 is significant.
  • Electrolytes: Assess for potassium shifts and calculate corrected sodium.
  • Ancillary Tests: ECG, CBC, urinalysis, and cultures to find precipitating cause (e.g., infection, MI).

Interactive Treatment Protocol

DKA management is a time-sensitive, protocol-driven process requiring meticulous attention to fluids, insulin, and electrolytes. This interactive timeline breaks down the standard treatment algorithm into key phases. Click on each step to reveal the specific actions, rationales, and critical considerations for that phase of care.

Hour 0: Initial Resuscitation & Assessment

Action: Secure IV access (x2), draw initial labs (glucose, electrolytes, VBG, ketones), obtain ECG, and administer 1.0-1.5 L of 0.9% NaCl over the first hour.

Rationale: The immediate priority is to restore intravascular volume, improve tissue perfusion, and begin lowering counterregulatory hormones. This is the most critical first step.

Hour 1: Insulin Initiation (with caution)

CRITICAL CHECK: Confirm serum potassium is ≥3.3 mEq/L before starting insulin. If it's low, hold insulin and aggressively replace potassium first.

Action: If K+ is adequate, start a continuous IV infusion of regular insulin at 0.1 units/kg/hr.

Rationale: Insulin is the definitive treatment to stop ketogenesis and reverse acidosis. Starting it in a hypokalemic state can be fatal.

Ongoing: Electrolyte Management

Action: Monitor electrolytes every 2-4 hours. If serum K+ is 3.3-5.2 mEq/L, proactively add 20-40 mEq of potassium to each liter of IV fluid.

Rationale: To prevent iatrogenic hypokalemia as insulin drives potassium into cells. The goal is to maintain serum K+ between 4.0-5.0 mEq/L.

Bicarbonate: Routine use is NOT recommended. Consider only in extreme, life-threatening acidosis (pH < 6.9).

Transition Point: Glucose ~200-250 mg/dL

Action: Change IV fluids to D5/0.45% NaCl (or similar dextrose-containing fluid). Reduce insulin infusion rate to 0.02-0.05 units/kg/hr.

Rationale: This crucial step prevents hypoglycemia while allowing the insulin infusion to continue at a rate sufficient to resolve the underlying ketosis, which clears more slowly than hyperglycemia.

Resolution & Transition to Subcutaneous Insulin

Resolution Criteria: Glucose <200, Bicarb ≥18, pH ≥7.3, Anion Gap closed (≤12).

CRITICAL OVERLAP: Administer a dose of basal (long-acting) subcutaneous insulin 1-2 hours BEFORE stopping the IV insulin infusion.

Rationale: Failure to overlap will result in rebound hyperglycemia and a recurrence of ketosis as the short-acting IV insulin wears off.

Complications & Prognosis

While acute mortality from DKA is low with modern treatment, the event is not benign. It carries risks for severe acute complications and is a powerful predictor of long-term morbidity and mortality. This section highlights the most critical risks to understand that a DKA admission is a sentinel event identifying a high-risk patient.

Acute Complications

  • Cerebral Edema

    The most feared complication, especially in children. Rare but carries high mortality (21-24%). Watch for headache, vomiting, and declining mental status during treatment.

  • Hypokalemia & Hypoglycemia

    Common iatrogenic risks from treatment. Require meticulous monitoring to prevent cardiac arrhythmias and neurological damage.

  • Thromboembolism

    DKA creates a hypercoagulable state. Consider prophylaxis in high-risk adults to prevent DVT, PE, and stroke.

DKA as a Sentinel Event for Long-Term Risk

A history of DKA is strongly associated with increased future mortality, independent of average glycemic control (HbA1c).

What is DKA? The Body's "Fuel Crisis"

This section explains DKA using a simple analogy. Understanding what happens inside your body during DKA is the first step to preventing this serious condition. Think of it as a fuel crisis where your body's cells are starving, even when there's plenty of sugar in your blood.

🔑

1. The Missing Key

Insulin is the "key" that lets sugar (fuel) into your body's cells. In DKA, there isn't enough insulin, so the sugar gets locked out and builds up in your blood.

🔥

2. Emergency Fuel

Because your cells are starving, your body panics and starts burning fat for fuel at a very high rate. This is its emergency backup plan.

☠️

3. Toxic Waste

Burning fat this way creates a toxic, acidic waste product called **ketones**. These ketones build up and poison your blood, making you very sick.

Warning Signs & "Sick Day Rules"

Being sick is the most common trigger for DKA. Having a clear plan—known as "Sick Day Rules"—is your best defense. This section covers the key symptoms to watch for and the essential steps you must take when you are ill to stay safe.

Know the Warning Signs

  • ●Intense thirst and very frequent urination
  • ●Nausea, vomiting, or stomach pain
  • ●Deep, rapid breathing or shortness of breath
  • ●Fruity-smelling breath
  • ●Feeling very tired, weak, or confused

Your Sick Day Action Plan

1. NEVER Stop Your Insulin

Your body needs insulin even more when you're sick. Talk to your doctor about how to adjust your dose.

2. Check Blood Sugar & Ketones

Check sugar every 3-4 hours. Check for ketones if your sugar is high (e.g., >240 mg/dL) or you feel sick.

3. Stay Hydrated

Sip sugar-free fluids like water or broth constantly to avoid dehydration.

When to Get Help: An Emergency Guide

DKA is a medical emergency. It is critical to know when you can manage symptoms with your doctor's help and when you must go to the emergency room immediately. Do not try to manage severe symptoms at home.

📞 Call Your Doctor If...

  • Your blood sugar stays high despite taking extra insulin.
  • Your ketone levels are "moderate" and not improving.
  • You are vomiting but can still keep some liquids down.
  • You are unsure what to do.

🚨 Go to the ER Immediately If...

  • Your ketones are "high" or "large".
  • You are vomiting and cannot keep any fluids down.
  • You have severe stomach pain or difficulty breathing.
  • You feel very sleepy, drowsy, or confused.
  • Do not drive yourself. Call for an ambulance.
