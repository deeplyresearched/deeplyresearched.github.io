What is a Seizure?

A seizure is a sudden, uncontrolled burst of electrical activity in the brain. Think of it like a brief electrical storm that temporarily disrupts normal brain function. This can change your behavior, movements, feelings, and level of consciousness. It's important to know that having a single seizure doesn't mean you have epilepsy.

Seizure vs. Epilepsy

A **seizure** is a single event. **Epilepsy** is a chronic neurological condition diagnosed after a person has had at least two unprovoked seizures more than 24 hours apart. Epilepsy means there is an underlying tendency to have recurrent seizures. This distinction is crucial because it guides long-term treatment decisions.

Diagnosis & Mimics

Diagnosing a seizure involves a careful process to determine if the event was truly epileptic and to rule out other conditions that can look similar. Below, you can explore the diagnostic steps and compare features of seizures with common mimics.

1. Clinical History

The most crucial step. A detailed description of the event from the patient and witnesses helps understand what happened before, during, and after the episode.

2. EEG

An electroencephalogram (EEG) records the brain's electrical activity. It can detect abnormal patterns (epileptiform discharges) that suggest a risk for future seizures.

3. Neuroimaging

An MRI is the preferred method to look for structural causes, such as a brain tumor, stroke, or scar tissue, that could be the source of seizures.
