The Big Picture: UK-EU Trade Post-Brexit

Since the UK's departure from the EU, new trade barriers have altered long-standing economic relationships. This has led to significant shifts in trade flows, particularly impacting the UK's trade balance with its largest trading partner. A Tale of Two Sectors: Goods vs. Services

The post-Brexit trade agreement (TCA) primarily focused on tariff-free trade in goods, but introduced significant non-tariff barriers like customs checks. Conversely, the services sector, a cornerstone of the UK economy, lost its seamless access to the EU single market. This section compares the divergent impacts on these two critical areas.

Goods Trade Impact

Increased administrative hurdles and supply chain friction have notably suppressed UK goods exports to the EU compared to a scenario without Brexit.

Services Trade Impact

While services trade has shown more resilience, the loss of 'passporting' rights has forced many financial and professional services firms to restructure operations.

Key Takeaways

1

The UK's trade deficit with the EU, especially in goods, has visibly widened post-Brexit due to non-tariff barriers impacting exports more than imports.

2

The UK's services sector has been more resilient but faces long-term uncertainty due to the loss of frictionless access to the EU single market.

3

While trade with non-EU countries has grown, it has not yet fully compensated for the reduction in trade intensity with the UK's closest and largest trading partner.
