A Nation Reshaped

On September 21, 1972, President Ferdinand Marcos placed the Philippines under Martial Law with Proclamation 1081, initiating a 14-year authoritarian rule. This act fundamentally altered the nation's political, social, and economic fabric. This infographic explores the systematic changes imposed during this period and analyzes their deep, lasting impact on Philippine democracy that continues to reverberate decades later.

The Deconstruction of Democracy

The declaration of Martial Law began a systematic process to consolidate power. Key democratic institutions were swiftly dismantled or subjugated, concentrating authority in the executive. Philippine External Debt (1972-1986)

The nation's external debt skyrocketed by over 1200% under the Marcos regime, growing from $2.2 billion to $28.3 billion, leaving a legacy of economic burden for subsequent generations.

The Façade of Elections

To maintain a veneer of legitimacy, the Marcos regime held several electoral exercises. However, these were conducted in a climate of fear with a controlled media. The Enduring Legacy

The fall of the Marcos regime did not erase its deep-seated impact. Systemic problems that plague the Philippines today—political dynasties, a culture of impunity, and weakened state institutions—have their roots in the Martial Law era.
