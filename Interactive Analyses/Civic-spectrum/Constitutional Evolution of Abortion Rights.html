Constitutional Evolution of Abortion Rights

From Roe v. Wade to Dobbs v. Jackson

This section provides a high-level overview of the 49-year trajectory of the constitutional right to abortion in the United States. It began with its establishment as a fundamental right in Roe v. Wade, evolved and was partially eroded by the "undue burden" standard in Planned Parenthood v. Casey, and was ultimately overturned in Dobbs v. Jackson, which returned regulatory power to individual states.

In 1973, Roe v. Wade established a nationwide, fundamental right to abortion. The Supreme Court ruled that a woman's right to make this decision was protected by the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause, which guarantees a "right to privacy." This decision invalidated many state laws that had criminalized abortion.

The Trimester Framework

To balance the individual's privacy right with the state's interest in maternal health and potential life, the Court created a three-part framework based on pregnancy trimesters.

First Trimester

The abortion decision is left to the pregnant woman and her physician. The state has no power to interfere.

Second Trimester

The state may regulate the abortion procedure in ways reasonably related to protecting maternal health (e.g., licensing clinics).

Third Trimester

Once the fetus is "viable" (can survive outside the womb), the state can regulate or even ban abortion, except when necessary to save the life or health of the mother.

Core Legal Principle

The right was deemed "fundamental," which meant that any state law restricting it was subject to **"Strict Scrutiny,"** the highest level of judicial review. This required the state to prove it had a "compelling interest" and that the law was "narrowly tailored" to achieve that interest. This was a very high bar for states to meet.

In 1992, Planned Parenthood v. Casey was a pivotal moment of both evolution and erosion. The Court, in a plurality opinion, faced a direct challenge to Roe but chose a middle path. It explicitly reaffirmed Roe's "central holding"—the constitutional right to an abortion before viability—but simultaneously dismantled the legal framework that protected it.

The "Undue Burden" Standard

Casey discarded Roe's trimester framework and "strict scrutiny" test. It replaced them with the new **"undue burden"** standard. This new test asks a different question:

"An undue burden exists and therefore a provision of law is invalid, if its purpose or effect is to place a substantial obstacle in the path of a woman seeking an abortion before the fetus attains viability."

This was a significant "erosion" because it lowered the bar for state regulations. As long as a restriction did not create a "substantial obstacle," it was permitted. This opened the door for numerous state-level restrictions that had been unconstitutional under Roe, such as mandatory 24-hour waiting periods and informed consent scripts.

Application in Casey

Applying this new standard, the Court upheld most of the Pennsylvania laws it was reviewing (like waiting periods and parental consent) but struck down one:

  • Upheld (Not an Undue Burden): 24-hour waiting period, informed consent requirements, parental consent (with a judicial bypass option).
  • Struck Down (Was an Undue Burden): A spousal notification requirement, which the Court found could endanger women in abusive relationships.

In 2022, Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization directly addressed a Mississippi law banning most abortions after 15 weeks. The Supreme Court's 6-3 decision not only upheld the law but took the definitive step of overturning both Roe v. Wade and Planned Parenthood v. Casey.

The Ruling: Overturning Precedent

The Court's majority opinion, authored by Justice Alito, declared that the Constitution does not confer a right to abortion. The decision explicitly stated that Roe and Casey "must be overruled" because they were "egregiously wrong" and their reasoning was "exceptionally weak."

The immediate effect of this ruling was the dissolution of nearly 50 years of federal constitutional protection for abortion. The authority to regulate or ban abortion was "returned to the people and their elected representatives," meaning the issue is now decided on a state-by-state basis. The legal standard for reviewing abortion laws was lowered from Casey's "undue burden" to the far weaker "rational-basis review."

The Dobbs case hinged on a direct clash of constitutional interpretations. Mississippi (Dobbs) argued for the right of states to regulate, while the last-remaining Mississippi clinic (Jackson) argued for the established constitutional right.

Petitioner's Argument (Dobbs / Mississippi)

  • The Constitution makes no express reference to a right to abortion.
  • The right is not "deeply rooted in U.S. history and tradition." They argued that at the time the 14th Amendment was adopted, three-quarters of states had criminalized abortion.
  • The "viability line" established by Roe and Casey is arbitrary, subjective, and prevents states from protecting their legitimate interest in "potential life."
  • States should be free to ban abortions if the ban is "rationally related to legitimate government interests."

Respondent's Argument (Jackson Women's Health)

  • The right to abortion is grounded in the 14th Amendment's protection of "liberty," which includes physical autonomy and bodily integrity.
  • This right is an essential element of liberty, similar to other protected rights like contraception and marriage.
  • For 50 years, the Court had recognized that the right to "possession of their own body" is a core part of liberty.
  • Upholding the Mississippi law would require overturning Roe and Casey, a move that would damage the Court's legitimacy and defy the principle of stare decisis (respect for precedent).

Between Roe (1973) and Casey (1992), and continuing until Dobbs (2022), the Supreme Court heard numerous cases that tested the boundaries of the Roe framework. These cases show the constant legal challenges that slowly chipped away at the broad right established in 1973.

Planned Parenthood v. Danforth (1976)

The Court struck down a law requiring spousal consent for an abortion, reinforcing the individual's right.

City of Akron v. Akron Center for Reproductive Health (1983)

The Court invalidated a wide range of restrictions, including a waiting period and a ban on abortions outside hospitals after the first trimester, seen as a strong reaffirmation of Roe.

Webster v. Reproductive Health Services (1989)

This was a major case where the Court upheld several restrictions, including a ban on state employees performing abortions and a viability test at 20 weeks. This decision signaled a weakening of Roe and set the stage for Casey.

Stenberg v. Carhart (2000)

The Court struck down a Nebraska ban on a specific abortion procedure ("dilation and extraction"), ruling it was too vague and lacked a necessary exception to protect the woman's health. This was a post-Casey application of the "undue burden" standard.

Gonzales v. Carhart (2007)

In a reversal from Stenberg, a slightly changed Court upheld a *federal* ban on the same procedure. This decision was significant for *not* containing a clear health exception, signaling a greater willingness to defer to legislative restrictions.

A major legal battleground, separate from the right itself, involved public funding and consent laws. Opponents of abortion sought to limit access by restricting Medicaid funding and requiring parental or spousal notification.

Maher v. Roe (1979)

The Court held that states could exclude abortion services from Medicaid coverage, ruling that the right to an abortion did not include a right to have it publicly funded. This created a significant barrier for low-income individuals.

Harris v. McRae (1980)

The Court upheld the "Hyde Amendment," a federal law that prohibited federal funding for abortions except when the woman's life was at risk or in cases of rape or incest. This confirmed the principle from Maher at the federal level.

H.L. v. Matheson (1981)

The Court upheld a law requiring parental notification for minors, as long as a "judicial bypass" procedure was available (allowing a minor to get a judge's permission instead).

Planned Parenthood v. Danforth (1976)

While allowing parental consent (with judicial bypass), the Court struck down a provision requiring a married woman to obtain spousal consent, ruling it gave the husband an unconstitutional veto over her decision.

This section provides a detailed elaboration of the key legal standards that defined the 50-year arc of abortion law. The shift from "Strict Scrutiny" to "Undue Burden" and finally to "Rational Basis" represents the core of the legal evolution and erosion of the right.

1. Strict Scrutiny (The Roe Standard)

This is the highest and most difficult standard in constitutional law. When Roe v. Wade declared abortion a "fundamental right," it automatically triggered this test for any law that tried to restrict it. To be constitutional, the state had to prove two things: (1) it had a "compelling state interest" (like protecting maternal health or potential life at viability) and (2) the law was "narrowly tailored" to achieve that interest, meaning it was the least restrictive means possible. This high bar is why most pre-viability abortion restrictions were struck down for 19 years.

2. The Undue Burden Standard (The Casey Standard)

Planned Parenthood v. Casey explicitly rejected Roe's rigid trimester framework and its "strict scrutiny" test. In its place, the Court created this new, weaker standard. A law was now unconstitutional *only if* it placed a "substantial obstacle" in the path of a woman seeking an abortion before viability. This was the great "erosion": it meant states *could* regulate abortion (e.g., with 24-hour waiting periods, mandatory counseling) as long as those regulations didn't amount to a "substantial obstacle." The Court found that informing a woman and making her wait 24 hours was not a substantial obstacle, but forcing her to notify a potentially abusive husband was.

3. The "Deeply Rooted in History" Test (The Dobbs Justification)

This is not a standard of review, but rather the test the Dobbs majority used to *remove* the right entirely. To be protected under the 14th Amendment's "liberty" clause, the Court said a right must be "deeply rooted in this Nation's history and tradition." The majority looked at US law in 1868 (when the 14th Amendment was ratified) and found that most states criminalized abortion. They concluded that because the right wasn't "deeply rooted" in 19th-century history, it was not a valid constitutional right, and Roe and Casey had been "egregiously wrong" to invent it.

4. Rational-Basis Review (The Post-Dobbs Standard)

This is the lowest and easiest standard of review in constitutional law. By declaring that abortion is *not* a fundamental right, the Dobbs Court automatically lowered the standard for review to this level. Now, an abortion law will be upheld as long as the state can provide *any* "legitimate government interest" (like protecting "potential life" at any stage) and the law is "rationally related" to that interest. This gives state laws a "strong presumption of validity," making them very easy to defend and very difficult to challenge.

At the heart of the 50-year legal battle is a fundamental disagreement over the meaning of "liberty" in the 14th Amendment, which states no person shall be deprived of "life, liberty, or property, without due process of law."

The Roe and Casey Interpretation: Liberty as Autonomy

The Roe court found that this "liberty" guarantee included a fundamental right to privacy, which covered personal decisions about family, marriage, contraception, and child-rearing. They ruled that this "is broad enough to encompass a woman's decision whether or not to terminate her pregnancy." Casey reaffirmed this, stating:

"These matters, involving the most intimate and personal choices a person may make in a lifetime, choices central to personal dignity and autonomy, are central to the liberty protected by the Fourteenth Amendment."

In this view, liberty includes the right to bodily integrity and the freedom to make life-defining decisions without government interference.

The Dobbs Interpretation: Liberty as History

The Dobbs majority rejected this broad view of "liberty." They argued that the 14th Amendment only protects rights that are either (1) explicitly mentioned in the Constitution or (2) "deeply rooted in this Nation's history and tradition." Since abortion was not explicitly mentioned and was criminalized in many states in 1868, they found it did not qualify.

The majority opinion also argued that defining "liberty" as "autonomy" was too broad and dangerous, stating that such a definition would "license fundamental rights to illicit drug use, [or] prostitution."

"We hold that Roe and Casey must be overruled. The Constitution makes no reference to abortion, and no such right is implicitly protected by any constitutional provision..."

The Dobbs decision was met with a rare joint dissent from Justices Breyer, Sotomayor, and Kagan. They argued that the majority's decision to overturn 50 years of precedent was a radical act that would have severe consequences.

The Joint Dissent's Arguments

The dissenters did not just defend the right to abortion; they criticized the majority's legal reasoning and its disregard for stare decisis (the principle of respecting settled precedent).

  • Erosion of Liberty: They argued that the majority's decision "discards a balance" and "says that from the very moment of fertilization, a woman has no rights to speak of."
  • Undermining the Court: They warned that overturning such a major, long-standing precedent for no reason other than a change in the Court's membership "undermines the Court's legitimacy."
  • Impact on Women: They stated the ruling would undermine women's "promise of freedom and equality," forcing them to carry pregnancies against their will, which has a massive impact on their "educational, economic, and political" lives.

Jeopardizing Other Rights

A major concern raised by the dissenters was that the majority's "deeply rooted in history" test endangers other established rights that are *also* based on the 14th Amendment's "liberty" clause but are not "deeply rooted" in 1868 history. These include:

  • The right to buy and use contraception (Griswold v. Connecticut)
  • The right to same-sex intimacy (Lawrence v. Texas)
  • The right to same-sex marriage (Obergefell v. Hodges)

While the majority opinion stated the decision was limited to abortion, Justice Thomas wrote a separate concurrence explicitly calling for the Court to "reconsider" all of these other precedents.

14th Amendment (Due Process Clause) +

States that no state shall "deprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law." This clause was the foundation for the "right to privacy" cited in Roe.

Strict Scrutiny (The Roe Standard) +

The highest level of judicial review. Requires the government to prove a law is "narrowly tailored" to serve a "compelling state interest." Applied to "fundamental rights."

Undue Burden (The Casey Standard) +

A weaker standard from Casey. A law is unconstitutional only if it places a "substantial obstacle" in the path of a woman seeking an abortion before viability.

"Deeply Rooted in History" (The Dobbs Test) +

The Dobbs majority test for unwritten rights. To be protected by the 14th Amendment, the right must have been recognized in 1868. The Court found abortion was not.

Rational-Basis Review (The Post-Dobbs Standard) +

The lowest level of judicial review. A law is upheld if it is "rationally related" to *any* "legitimate government interest." Gives laws a strong presumption of validity.
