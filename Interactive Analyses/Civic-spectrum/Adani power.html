The Adani Group & India's Energy Transition

This analysis explores the central conflict of the Adani Group's role in India's energy sector: its simultaneous dominance in thermal power and its massive investment in green energy. We examine to what extent this duality supports or challenges India's ambitious climate goals, especially in light of significant ESG controversies.

To understand Adani's role, we must first understand India's national energy landscape. The country is at a pivotal moment, with its total installed power capacity almost evenly split between fossil fuels and non-fossil fuel sources. This 50/50 balance is the stage upon which Adani's corporate strategy unfolds.

Key Concepts in the Energy Debate

To understand the complexities of this topic, it's helpful to define the key terms and concepts that shape the business strategies and national policies involved.

The Energy Transition refers to the global shift from an energy system based on fossil fuels (like coal and gas) to one based on renewable energy sources (like solar and wind). For India, this means decarbonizing its rapidly growing economy, investing in green technology, and meeting its international climate commitments, such as the "Net-Zero by 2070" target.

Thermal power provides "baseload power," which is stable, reliable electricity that can be dispatched 24/7, regardless of weather. As India's economy and electricity demand grow, it needs this affordable and reliable power to prevent shortages and stabilize the grid. While renewables are cleaner, their output is intermittent (solar only works in the day, wind is variable), creating a need for a stable power source to fill the gaps. Adani Power's new thermal plants are justified on this basis of energy security.

ESG refers to three central factors used by investors to measure a company's sustainability and ethical impact.
• Environmental: A company's impact on the planet (e.g., carbon emissions, water use).
• Social: How a company treats its employees, customers, and the communities it operates in.
• Governance: How a company is run (e.g., board independence, accounting practices, anti-corruption).
Poor ESG scores or controversies (like the Hindenburg report) can scare away "green" investors and make borrowing money more expensive.

A PPA is a long-term contract between an electricity generator (like Adani Power or Adani Green) and a buyer (usually a state-owned utility). The generator builds and operates a power plant, and the utility agrees to buy a set amount of power for a fixed price over a long period (e.g., 25 years). This provides stable, predictable revenue for the generator, making it possible to secure financing for large, expensive projects like solar farms or thermal plants.

India's Ambitious National Transition

The Adani Group's strategy operates within the context of India's aggressive national climate goals. The government has set a clear and ambitious target for 2030, aiming to fundamentally shift its energy mix away from fossil fuels. This national ambition is the benchmark against which corporate actions are measured.

India's Path to 2030

India has pledged to achieve 500 GW of non-fossil fuel-based energy capacity by 2030. Having already reached nearly half of that, the nation must still more than double its current non-fossil capacity in the coming years, a monumental industrial undertaking.

National Context: A Deeper Look

India's energy transition is not just a plan; it's a massive industrial shift that has already placed the country as a global leader in renewables, even as it faces significant financial and manufacturing challenges.

Global Renewable Rankings

India has established itself as a major player in the global renewable energy landscape, ranking highly in key areas of deployment.

Domestic Manufacturing Boost

A key part of India's strategy is "Make in India" – building its own components to reduce reliance on imports and secure the supply chain.

  • ⇡ Solar PV Module Capacity: Surged 38-fold, from 2.3 GW to 88 GW (2014-2025).
  • ⇡ Solar PV Cell Capacity: Grew 21-fold, from 1.2 GW to 25 GW (2014-2025).
  • ⇡ Wind Turbine Manufacturing: 18 GW of annual manufacturing capacity is now available domestically.

Adani's Duality: Green Giant & Thermal Pillar

The core of the "Adani paradox" lies in its two major energy arms. Adani Green Energy Ltd (AGEL) is building one of the world's largest renewable portfolios, while Adani Power Ltd (APL) remains India's largest private thermal producer and is still expanding its coal-based capacity. This section explores this fundamental contradiction.

The Green Giant (AGEL)

Adani Green Energy is executing a monumental green expansion, positioning itself as a primary driver of India's renewable targets.

  • ✓ Ambitious Target: Aiming for 50 GW of renewable energy capacity by 2030.
  • ✓ World's Largest RE Park: Building a 30 GW renewable energy park in Khavda, Gujarat.
  • ✓ Rapid Growth: Operational capacity reached over 16.7 GW as of late 2025, with a $100bn investment planned in energy transition.

The Thermal Pillar (APL)

Simultaneously, Adani Power maintains and even expands its thermal footprint, citing India's need for stable, baseload power.

  • ⚠ Market Dominance: Remains India's largest private-sector thermal power producer.
  • ⚠ New Thermal Expansion: Recently won a bid to set up a new 1,496 MW (1.5 GW) ultra-supercritical thermal plant.
  • ⚠ Controversial Projects: Operates controversial coal mining projects that are a focus of ESG concerns.

Deep Dive: Adani Green Energy (AGEL)

AGEL is the group's flagship for its green ambitions. Its strategy is defined by massive scale, rapid deployment of new technology, and strong financial performance within the renewables sector.

Operational & Financial Snapshot

AGEL has demonstrated significant year-on-year growth, reinforcing its market-leading position and financial health.

AGEL reported an industry-leading EBITDA margin of 91.7%, showing high profitability from its green assets.

Deep Dive: Adani Power (APL)

Adani Power remains the group's foundation in conventional energy. As India's largest private thermal producer, its role is to provide the stable, 24/7 baseload power that the growing economy demands. Its strategy includes both building new, more efficient plants and efforts to reduce the emissions of its existing fleet.

Key Role & New Expansion

New Thermal Project

APL is not just maintaining its fleet; it is actively expanding it with new-generation thermal technology.

  • ⚡ Capacity: 1,496 MW (1.5 GW)
  • 📅 PPA: 25-year contract with Maharashtra (MSEDCL).
  • 🔧 Technology: Ultra-supercritical, which is more efficient and has lower emissions than older coal plants.

Mitigation Efforts

To address its environmental impact, APL is exploring technologies to reduce the carbon intensity of its thermal generation.

  • 🍂 Biomass Blending: Plans to co-fire biomass (like crop residue) with coal to reduce net CO2 emissions.
  • 💧 Future Pilots: Investigating pilots for co-firing hydrogen and green ammonia, as well as carbon capture.

The ESG Investor's Dilemma

Adani Green (AGEL) is a pure-play green investment, yet its parent group faces severe governance allegations, creating a paradox for ESG funds.

  • 🧐 Widespread Holding: Approximately 770 ESG-labeled funds hold shares of Adani Green Energy.
  • 🧐 The Conflict: These funds are invested in one of the world's largest green projects, but are exposed to a group accused of bribery and fraud.
  • 🧐 The Risk: This situation "calls into question" the ability of ESG funds to properly screen for governance ("G") risks, especially in emerging markets.

ESG & Governance Controversies

The conflict is not just about carbon. A significant part of the challenge to Adani's "green" narrative comes from persistent Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) controversies. These issues raise serious questions about corporate practices and risk, complicating the group's standing with global ESG-focused investors.

Governance: A Crisis of Confidence

Governance has been the most prominent area of concern, shaking investor confidence and leading to tangible consequences from ratings agencies.

  • Hindenburg Allegations: The 2023 short-seller report alleged decades of stock manipulation and accounting fraud, which the Adani Group denies.
  • Bribery Allegations: In 2024, US prosecutors reportedly expanded a probe to include allegations that an Adani Group entity engaged in bribery to secure solar contracts.
  • Ratings Downgrades: In response to these events, major ESG rating agencies like MSCI and Sustainalytics downgraded their scores for several Adani companies, flagging severe governance risks.

Environmental: A Mixed Record

The group's environmental record is a study in contrasts. While AGEL promotes its positive metrics, APL faces criticism for its core business.

  • AGEL's Claims: Adani Green Energy reports positive metrics like being 100% water-positive at its large plants and achieving zero-waste-to-landfill status.
  • APL's Impact: Adani Power's operations are linked to "some of the most controversial coal mining projects in recent history," which carry significant environmental footprints, carbon emissions, and local pollution risks.

Social: Community & Land Impact

Large-scale energy and infrastructure projects invariably have significant social impacts. Concerns in this area typically revolve around the acquisition of land and the effect on local communities.

  • Land Acquisition: Both large-scale thermal and massive solar/wind parks require vast tracts of land, often leading to disputes with local communities and farmers.
  • Livelihood Disruption: Activist groups have raised concerns about the impact of port and power plant operations on traditional livelihoods, such as fishing communities.

The Future: Green Hydrogen

Beyond solar and wind, the Adani Group has identified green hydrogen as a "natural adjacency" and a key part of its $100bn transition investment. The goal is to leverage its massive renewable energy capacity to produce the "world's least expensive green electron," which can then be used to create green hydrogen, a clean fuel with the potential to decarbonize heavy industry and even turn India into an energy exporter.

Synthesis: Conflict or Pragmatism?

To what extent does Adani's thermal dominance conflict with India's green transition? The answer is not a simple "yes" or "no." The group's strategy is a "microcosm of India" itself, embodying the nation's complex, and at times contradictory, path to development. Below are the two primary arguments.

The "Inherent Conflict" Argument

This view holds that the group's actions are fundamentally contradictory and that the thermal portfolio poses a significant challenge.

  • Carbon Lock-in: Every new thermal plant, like the 1.5 GW project, locks in decades of carbon emissions, directly conflicting with net-zero goals.
  • Capital Diversion: Capital and resources allocated to new coal projects could be exclusively focused on accelerating green hydrogen and renewable deployment.
  • Reputational Contagion: The severe governance and environmental controversies (bribery, fraud allegations) tarnish the "green" brand, making it harder for AGEL to attract premium ESG-focused global capital.
  • Conflicting Signals: It sends mixed signals to the market, positioning the group as a "transition" leader while simultaneously being a primary purveyor of the legacy problem.

The "Pragmatic Parallel" Argument

This view, often shared by the company and some analysts, is that the two strategies are parallel and necessary for India's unique growth story.

  • Energy Security: India's demand for power is rising rapidly. It needs cheap, reliable baseload power *today* to fuel its economy, a role that only thermal power can currently fill at scale.
  • Fueling the Transition: The stable revenue and cash flow from the profitable thermal business (APL) can be used to finance the massive, capital-intensive build-out of the green energy business (AGEL).
  • Microcosm of India: The group is simply reflecting India's national policy, which also involves building new renewables (500 GW target) while still relying on coal for baseload security.
  • Massive Green Scale: The $100bn investment and 30 GW Khavda park are so large that they will undeniably accelerate India's transition, regardless of the thermal holdings.
