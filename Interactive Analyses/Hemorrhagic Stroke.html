A "Burst Pipe" in the Brain

Hemorrhagic stroke occurs when a blood vessel ruptures, causing bleeding into or around the brain. Unlike ischemic strokes caused by clots, this type is a catastrophic bleed that puts immense pressure on brain tissue, leading to severe damage. It is less common but far more deadly.

Key Treatment Modalities

Aggressive BP Control

Rapidly lowering systolic blood pressure to a target of 140 mmHg using IV medications is crucial to prevent the hematoma from expanding.

Endovascular Coiling

A minimally invasive procedure for aneurysms. A catheter is used to place platinum coils inside the aneurysm, causing a clot to form and sealing it off from circulation.

Minimally Invasive Surgery

For certain ICH cases, a neuroendoscope or stereotactic catheter can be used to evacuate the blood clot, relieving pressure with less damage to surrounding brain tissue.

Reversal of Anticoagulants

For patients on blood thinners, specific reversal agents (e.g., Idarucizumab, Andexanet Alfa) or concentrates (PCC) are given immediately to restore normal clotting.

Neurosurgical Clipping

An open brain surgery where a small metal clip is placed across the neck of an aneurysm to block normal blood flow from entering it.

Nimodipine for SAH

The only drug proven to improve outcomes after SAH. It is given for 21 days to prevent delayed complications from arterial narrowing (vasospasm).

Recovery, Prognosis & Life After Stroke

The road to recovery is long and challenging, with a high risk of long-term disability. A dedicated, multidisciplinary rehabilitation team is essential to help survivors regain function and independence.

30-Day Mortality Rate

The acute prognosis for hemorrhagic stroke is grim, with ICH having a 30-day mortality rate of approximately 40%.

Common Long-Term Complications

  • ♿

    Motor & Sensory Deficits: Persistent weakness, paralysis, spasticity, or loss of sensation.

  • 🧠

    Cognitive Dysfunction: Problems with memory, attention, and executive function are very common.

  • 💬

    Communication Disorders: Difficulty speaking (aphasia) or slurred speech (dysarthria).

  • 😔

    Emotional Problems: High rates of depression, anxiety, and PTSD among survivors.
