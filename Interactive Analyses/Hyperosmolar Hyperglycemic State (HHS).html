HHS Management Protocol

HHS management is a delicate balance of restoring volume and gradually correcting osmolality. This protocol outlines a phased, goal-directed approach. The cornerstone of therapy is aggressive fluid resuscitation, which must precede insulin administration.

Pillar 1: Fluid Resuscitation

This is the most critical first step. The average fluid deficit is 9-12 liters. The goal is to restore circulatory volume and gradually lower osmolality by no more than 3 mOsm/kg/hr.

Initial Phase (First Hour)

  • Administer 1 - 1.5 L of 0.9% NaCl (isotonic saline) over the first hour.
  • Rate: 15-20 mL/kg/hr.
  • Goal: Stabilize hemodynamics.

Pillar 3: Insulin Therapy

Insulin is administered *after* fluid resuscitation has begun to avoid worsening intravascular volume depletion.

  • No initial bolus is recommended for pure HHS.
  • Start a continuous IV infusion at a low, fixed rate: 0.05 units/kg/hour. (Half the DKA dose).
  • Goal: Gradual glucose decline of 50-90 mg/dL per hour.
  • Avoid rapid drops in glucose to prevent cerebral edema.

HHS: Patient & Caregiver Guide

What is Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)?

HHS is a dangerous complication of diabetes, most common in people with type 2. Think of it like this: your blood becomes too thick with sugar, like syrup. This happens because there isn't enough insulin to move sugar from your blood into your cells for energy.

This "syrupy" blood makes your kidneys work overtime to get rid of the extra sugar, causing you to urinate a lot. This leads to extreme dehydration, which is the main danger of HHS. It develops slowly over days or weeks.

Warning Signs: When to Get Help Immediately

HHS is a medical emergency. If you or a loved one with diabetes experience these symptoms, especially during an illness like the flu or an infection, call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room.
